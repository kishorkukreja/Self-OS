    10|# Where the Transformation Begins: Creating a Consumer-Centric Supply Chain Strategy
    11|
    12|**Source:** McKinsey & Company  
    13|**Date:** December 23, 2021  
    14|**Authors:** Joyce Chai, Aniket Joglekar, Tim Lange, Kumar Venkataraman  
    15|**URL:** https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/retail/our-insights/where-the-transformation-begins-creating-a-consumer-centric-supply-chain-strategy
    16|
    17|---
    18|
    19|## Core Thesis
    20|
    21|> "One of the few constants of the consumer industry is change."
    22|
    23|The pandemic accelerated existing shifts (e-commerce growth, decline of shopping malls, unseating of mom-and-pop stores) and added new complexity. Consumers now expect **convenience, cost competitiveness, and fast delivery** across an omnichannel journey.
    24|
    25|> "All aspire to provide the right products sustainably and delivered at lowest cost possible, at the right speed, and with a great consumer experience. However, to succeed in the omnichannel world, the conventional one-size-fits-all approach will no longer be sufficient."
    26|
    27|**The key to winning** is a **segmented, consumer-back approach** that distinguishes offerings across consumer segments based on their requirements, product preferences, and locations—while adjusting the supply chain structure to enable that differentiation. This segmentation must also include **brick-and-mortar stores, wholesale partners, and marketplaces** to serve the end consumer holistically.
    28|
    29|---
    30|
    31|## The Three-Step Approach
    32|
    33|### Step 1: Define Target Consumer-Back Segments
    34|
    35|Segments must be defined as a combination of three intersecting factors:
    36|
    37|| Factor | Key Considerations |
    38||--------|-------------------|
    39|| **Consumer Requirements & Preferences** | Demographics, geography, product preferences. Are they cost-conscious or premium shoppers? Do they prioritize speed, sustainability, or flexibility? |
    40|| **Product Characteristics** | Velocity/volatility (e.g., seasonal demand ebbs/flows), product type/availability, replenishment flows, how demand tracks with segments. |
    41|| **Supply Chain Structure** | Current node structure, fulfillment methods. Which requirements can you deliver today? Which priority segments require structural change? |
    42|
    43|> "Providing optimal value in the omnichannel world will require tailored strategies for each part of this structure."
    44|
    45|---
    46|
    47|### Step 2: Define the 'North Star' Vision & Dimensions of Differentiation
    48|
    49|For each priority segment, companies must explicitly define what expectations to deliver to avoid costly mismatches (e.g., providing premium speed to price-driven consumers).
    50|
    51|#### Four Dimensions of Differentiation
    52|
    53|**1. Service** — Three factors with "hygiene" elements (table stakes) and competitive differentiators:
    54|- **Speed:** US standard is 3–5 days; EU is 2 days. *Differentiation examples:* Amazon Prime offers 1–2 day delivery for select products; Gorillas (US/Germany) offers ultrafast grocery delivery (**15 minutes or less**).
    55|- **Optionality & Flexibility:** Ability to choose/change fulfillment methods. *Example:* Target instituted curbside pickup during the pandemic and allows easy changes to pickup dates/times.
    56|- **Convenience:** Incremental offerings at varying price points. *Examples:* A major home-goods retailer offers white-glove assembly/installation services; Instacart allows paying for precise delivery windows, while DoorDash (Safeway partnership) dictates estimated times without precision.
    57|
    58|**2. Availability**
    59|- **End-of-life availability:** Maintain stock for loyal customers vs. cut production to reduce costs/complexity.
    60|- **Limited access:** Tier access to scarce products by customer lifetime value. *Example:* Luxury retailers deliberately limit high-demand products, offering them exclusively to established customers first.
    61|
    62|**3. Cost Effectiveness**
    63|Companies must take a **holistic view of the fully loaded cost** of delivering speed, free returns, fragmented product flows, and convenience—balancing consumer expectations against profitability.
    64|
    65|**4. Sustainability (ESG)**
    66|- **Survey data:** In McKinsey’s 2020 pulse survey on fashion sustainability, **57%** of respondents made significant lifestyle changes to lessen environmental impact, and **more than 60%** go out of their way to recycle and purchase environmentally friendly packaging.
    67|- Differentiation options: glass/multiuse packaging vs. disposable plastic, partnering only with sustainable/socially responsible suppliers (even at higher cost).
    68|
    69|---
    70|
    71|### Step 3: Create a Differentiated Supply Chain Strategy
    72|
    73|Four critical enablers:
    74|
    75|1. **Network & Ecosystem Strategy**
    76|   - Place the right nodes in the right locations (warehouses, stores, **dark stores**) to enable a multispeed supply chain.
    77|
    78|2. **World-Class Planning & Allocation**
    79|   - Allocate the right inventory to the right nodes based on segmented product flow; use geospatial demand data to place select SKUs rather than stocking every SKU in every location.
    80|
    81|3. **Omnichannel Fulfillment Capabilities**
    82|   - Deliver via consumer-preferred methods (e.g., BOPIS, delivery to door, ship-from-store).
    83|
    84|4. **Customer Experience & Sustainability**
    85|   - Build sustainability into operations (e.g., electric last-mile fleets, reusable packaging, carbon-offset delivery options).
