# Where the Transformation Begins: Creating a Consumer-Centric Supply Chain Strategy

**Source:** McKinsey & Company  
**Date:** December 23, 2021  
**Authors:** Joyce Chai, Aniket Joglekar, Tim Lange, Kumar Venkataraman  
**URL:** https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/retail/our-insights/where-the-transformation-begins-creating-a-consumer-centric-supply-chain-strategy

---

## Core Thesis

> "One of the few constants of the consumer industry is change."

The pandemic accelerated existing shifts (e-commerce growth, decline of shopping malls, unseating of mom-and-pop stores) and added new complexity. Consumers now expect **convenience, cost competitiveness, and fast delivery** across an omnichannel journey.

> "All aspire to provide the right products sustainably and delivered at lowest cost possible, at the right speed, and with a great consumer experience. However, to succeed in the omnichannel world, the conventional one-size-fits-all approach will no longer be sufficient."

**The key to winning** is a **segmented, consumer-back approach** that distinguishes offerings across consumer segments based on their requirements, product preferences, and locations—while adjusting the supply chain structure to enable that differentiation. This segmentation must also include **brick-and-mortar stores, wholesale partners, and marketplaces** to serve the end consumer holistically.

---

## The Three-Step Approach

### Step 1: Define Target Consumer-Back Segments

Segments must be defined as a combination of three intersecting factors:

| Factor | Key Considerations |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Consumer Requirements & Preferences** | Demographics, geography, product preferences. Are they cost-conscious or premium shoppers? Do they prioritize speed, sustainability, or flexibility? |
| **Product Characteristics** | Velocity/volatility (e.g., seasonal demand ebbs/flows), product type/availability, replenishment flows, how demand tracks with segments. |
| **Supply Chain Structure** | Current node structure, fulfillment methods. Which requirements can you deliver today? Which priority segments require structural change? |

> "Providing optimal value in the omnichannel world will require tailored strategies for each part of this structure."

---

### Step 2: Define the 'North Star' Vision & Dimensions of Differentiation

For each priority segment, companies must explicitly define what expectations to deliver to avoid costly mismatches (e.g., providing premium speed to price-driven consumers).

#### Four Dimensions of Differentiation

**1. Service** — Three factors with "hygiene" elements (table stakes) and competitive differentiators:
- **Speed:** US standard is 3–5 days; EU is 2 days. *Differentiation examples:* Amazon Prime offers 1–2 day delivery for select products; Gorillas (US/Germany) offers ultrafast grocery delivery (**15 minutes or less**).
- **Optionality & Flexibility:** Ability to choose/change fulfillment methods. *Example:* Target instituted curbside pickup during the pandemic and allows easy changes to pickup dates/times.
- **Convenience:** Incremental offerings at varying price points. *Examples:* A major home-goods retailer offers white-glove assembly/installation services; Instacart allows paying for precise delivery windows, while DoorDash (Safeway partnership) dictates estimated times without precision.

**2. Availability**
- **End-of-life availability:** Maintain stock for loyal customers vs. cut production to reduce costs/complexity.
- **Limited access:** Tier access to scarce products by customer lifetime value. *Example:* Luxury retailers deliberately limit high-demand products, offering them exclusively to established customers first.

**3. Cost Effectiveness**
Companies must take a **holistic view of the fully loaded cost** of delivering speed, free returns, fragmented product flows, and convenience—balancing consumer expectations against profitability.

**4. Sustainability (ESG)**
- **Survey data:** In McKinsey’s 2020 pulse survey on fashion sustainability, **57%** of respondents made significant lifestyle changes to lessen environmental impact, and **more than 60%** go out of their way to recycle and purchase environmentally friendly packaging.
- Differentiation options: glass/multiuse packaging vs. disposable plastic, partnering only with sustainable/socially responsible suppliers (even at higher cost).

---

### Step 3: Create a Differentiated Supply Chain Strategy

Four critical enablers:

1. **Network & Ecosystem Strategy**
   - Place the right nodes in the right locations (warehouses, stores, **dark stores**) to enable a multispeed supply chain.

2. **World-Class Planning & Allocation**
   - Allocate the right inventory to the right nodes based on segmented product flow; use geospatial demand data to place select SKUs rather than stocking every SKU in every location.

3. **Omnichannel Fulfillment Capabilities**
   - Deliver via consumer-preferred methods (e.g., BOPIS, delivery to door, ship-from-store).

4. **Customer Experience & Sustainability**
   - Build sustainability into operations (e.g., electric last-mile fleets, reusable packaging, carbon-offset delivery options).
