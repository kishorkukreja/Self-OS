    10|# Decision-Centric Supply Chain Planning: Outlook on 2026 Gartner Report
    11|
    12|**Source:** ToolsGroup analysis of *Gartner Magic Quadrant for Supply Chain Planning Solutions: Discrete Industries* (18 March 2026)
    13|
    14|## Executive Summary
    15|The 2026 Gartner report signals a fundamental rewrite of supply chain planning rules. Success is no longer measured by forecast accuracy or plan stability, but by the **quality, speed, and resilience of decisions** under uncertainty. In this model, planning becomes a continuous, adaptive decision system—a "decision repository"—augmented by AI but governed by human judgment.
    16|
    17|---
    18|
    19|## The Shift: Why Planning Is Being Re-Defined
    20|
    21|Traditional planning succeeded in stable environments where variability was low. Today, **"volatility is the new normal."** Demand shifts faster than planning cycles, and disruptions ripple across global networks in days.
    22|
    23|> *"Artificial intelligence (AI) isn't replacing human planners; instead, AI in supply chain planning is accelerating the shift by greatly expanding what can be sensed, analyzed, and adjusted in near real time."*
    24|
    25|AI raises the bar on decision quality—helping organizations recognize emerging patterns, explore complex trade-offs at scale, and intervene earlier.
    26|
    27|---
    28|
    29|## Planning as a Decision Repository
    30|
    31|A central idea from the 2026 report: treat planning as the **"decision repository"** for the end-to-end supply chain. Rather than a suite of separate tools (demand, supply, inventory), planning becomes a unified environment where decisions are evaluated, aligned, and governed across time horizons.
    32|
    33|Forecasts and plans become inputs into a broader decision context, supporting questions such as:
    34|- Which decisions matter most right now?
    35|- What objectives are we prioritizing under current conditions?
    36|- What risks are we accepting, and where?
    37|
    38|> *"Planning shifts from a periodic batch exercise to a continuous, adaptive decision system."*
    39|
    40|---
    41|
    42|## From Plan Outputs to Decision-Centric Planning
    43|
    44|Traditional systems asked: *What should the plan be?* Modern supply chains require answering:
    45|- What do we do when reality deviates from the plan?
    46|- Which tradeoffs are acceptable given current constraints?
    47|- How quickly can we respond without creating instability elsewhere?
    48|
    49|**Key insight:** Competitive advantage comes from superior decision-making when things change, not from computational accuracy or the "perfect" plan on paper.
    50|
    51|---
    52|
    53|## Re-Examining Inventory and Service
    54|
    55|The old mindset assumes a fixed tradeoff between inventory reduction and service levels. In reality, excess inventory and poor service often coexist because companies hold **"too much of the wrong stock in the wrong locations."**
    56|
    57|A decision-centric approach reframes inventory as a decision problem through three questions:
    58|
    59|1. **Where does variability truly exist?**  
    60|   Measure demand volatility, lead-time inconsistency, and execution risk explicitly rather than using one-size-fits-all safety stock.
    61|
    62|2. **Which products and customers drive value?**  
    63|   Differentiate service targets by business impact. Prioritize high-margin products and key customers; manage lower-priority items more tightly.
    64|
    65|3. **How should inventory be positioned across the network?**  
    66|   Optimize at the network level (not node by node) to leverage risk-pooling and avoid duplicated buffers.
    67|
    68|**Outcome:** Organizations frequently boost service levels *while* reducing overall inventory when policies are treated as living, continuously reviewed decisions.
    69|

    72|## Segmentation as a Living Decision Framework
    73|
    74|Segmentation is not a one-time A/B/C exercise—it guides daily tradeoffs. Different patterns require different rules:
    75|
    76|| Segment | Strategy |
    77||---------|----------|
    78|| **Stable, high-volume items** | Disciplined, regular replenishment; tighter safety buffers; focus on efficiency |
    79|| **Seasonal/promotional products** | Time-bound decisions tied to events; clear end-of-season strategies |
    80|| **Intermittent/long-tail SKUs** | Explicit choices about service commitments; alternate sourcing, on-demand manufacturing, or postponement |
    81|
    82|**Value:** When frontline teams understand *why* certain products are managed differently, decisions become faster, more transparent, and easier to defend.
    83|

    85|
    86|## Scenario-Driven Planning Becomes Operational
    87|
    88|By 2026, scenario planning is part of regular operations, not just annual strategy. Planners routinely pose *"what if"* questions:
    89|- What if demand surges 20% above forecast?
    90|- What if a critical supplier’s lead time doubles?
    91|- What if we relax service targets for a low-margin line?
    92|
    93|Evaluating impacts *before* acting reduces firefighting and builds confidence. It also creates a **shared language** across supply chain, finance, and commercial teams, turning implicit assumptions into explicit choices.
    94|
    95|---
    96|
    97|## AI as an Enabler of Better Decisions
    98|
    99|AI does not replace human judgment; it amplifies decision-centric planning through three contributions:
   100|
   101|1. **Pattern Recognition Amid Volatility:** AI detects anomalies and emerging demand patterns faster than manual analysis.
   102|2. **Trade-off Exploration at Scale:** Machine learning evaluates millions of inventory, service, and cost combinations to surface efficient frontiers.
   103|3. **Prescriptive Recommendations:** Rather than producing a single forecast number, AI recommends specific actions (e.g., shift 500 units from Node A to Node B) with expected outcomes.
