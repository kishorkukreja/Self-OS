# Supply Chain Signals Daily Research — 2026-05-11

## Top Moves Candidates

1. **U.S. tariff policy is back in motion through narrower legal lanes.** Reuters reported that the USTR opened a four-day Section 301 hearing on alleged excess industrial capacity across 16 major trading partners, including China, the EU, Japan, South Korea, Mexico, and Vietnam. Domestic producers want more duties; import-heavy sectors and agriculture groups are warning about higher costs and retaliation. Operator read: tariff risk has moved from broad emergency authority to targeted capacity, forced-labor, and sector probes. That makes the risk harder to model because exposure depends on product classification, origin, and legal route rather than one blanket rate. [Reuters, May 5]

2. **Manufacturing is expanding, but the input side is flashing red.** ISM's April Manufacturing PMI held at 52.7, the fourth straight month of expansion. The warning is inside the subindexes: prices rose to 84.6, supplier deliveries slowed to 60.6, employment fell to 46.4, customer inventories stayed too low at 39.1, and new export orders contracted at 47.9. Operator read: demand is not collapsing, but cost and lead-time risk are moving before capacity planners get much labor flexibility. [ISM Manufacturing PMI PDF, May 1]

3. **Services supply chains show the same inflation pattern.** ISM's April Services PMI came in at 53.6. Supplier deliveries slowed to 56.8, prices stayed at 70.7, and all 18 industries reported higher prices paid. The services report matters for supply chain because transport, construction, wholesale, software, labor, and facility services feed directly into operating cost. [ISM Services PMI, May 5]

4. **Pharma is treating tariff threat as a network-design problem.** Reuters reported that global drugmakers are expanding U.S. manufacturing, stockpiling inventory, and negotiating pricing or investment arrangements to reduce exposure to threatened 100% tariffs on branded drugs. Companies cited in the Reuters piece include Pfizer, AstraZeneca, Eli Lilly, Johnson & Johnson, Merck, Roche, GSK, Novo Nordisk, AbbVie, and Novartis. Operator read: this is not just compliance work; it is capacity placement, tech transfer, inventory positioning, and supplier qualification compressed into a policy window. [Reuters, May 4]

5. **Freight has split signals: domestic tightness, softer trans-Pacific spot rates.** FreightWaves' May State of the Industry landing page says spot and contract rates are rising in constrained truckload markets, long-term contract rates are up about 8% since last fall, and diesel volatility remains a rate-planning problem. A separate FreightWaves/Freightos item says Asia-U.S. West Coast ocean rates fell 21% in a week to $1,916/FEU, while Asia-U.S. East Coast rates fell 10% to $3,457/FEU. Operator read: don't read one freight index as the market. Ocean oversupply and cautious import planning can coexist with domestic capacity tightness and fuel-driven accessorial pressure. [FreightWaves, May 2026]

6. **CBP's tariff refund workflow is becoming an operational cash-flow issue.** Supply Chain Dive reported that CBP's CAPE portal, built inside ACE to process refunds for invalidated IEEPA tariffs, launched April 20 and could begin refunds as early as May 12. CBP had accepted about 21% of submitted entries and moved about 3% into the refund process as of the cited filing/update. Operator read: importers should treat this as a working-capital recovery workflow, not a back-office curiosity. Data quality, broker coordination, ACH readiness, and rejected-entry cleanup determine speed to cash. [Supply Chain Dive, Apr. 30/May 4 update]

## Problem Beneath the Headline Candidates

- **Policy volatility is turning supply chain design into legal-option management.** Tariff exposure now depends on Section 301 investigations, court rulings, refund portals, sector carve-outs, and country/product-level classification. The operator question: which SKUs have enough margin or strategic value to justify dual sourcing, tariff engineering, or inventory buffers?

- **The real squeeze is cost plus uncertainty, not a single bottleneck.** ISM manufacturing and services both show expansion with elevated prices and slower supplier deliveries. That combination punishes teams that plan only to demand signals. The operator question: which inputs need cost-index triggers, alternate lanes, or supplier escalation before the lead-time shock lands in customer service?

- **AI in logistics is moving from demo to asset orchestration.** FedEx is embedding AI into routing, capacity, predictive maintenance, robotics, and facility workflows. The operator question: which network decisions should be automated because the time window is too short for manual planning, and which still need human override?

Reuters' Section 301 item explicitly includes legacy semiconductors and strategic materials in the overcapacity debate. WTO's March outlook says AI-related goods demand boosted 2025 trade and could add 0.5 percentage points to 2026 merchandise trade growth if it stays strong. The chain can connect wafers, advanced packaging, HBM, networking, power, cooling, data center construction, and freight. Use it unless later-week news gives a stronger chain.

- **Crude to Plastic.** ISM and Reuters both point to energy-linked input costs, oil, resin, fuel, and logistics costs. This chain is useful if Middle East/Hormuz or resin/fertilizer signals dominate the rest of the week.

## From Cost Center to Advantage Candidates

- **Physical AI in parcel networks.** FedEx says it plans and optimizes 100,000 first-mile and last-mile routes daily with AI and aims to integrate AI into more than 50% of core operational workflows by 2028. Its MOBIUS predictive-maintenance platform has reportedly prevented 17,000 hours of potential downtime across 41 surface operations facilities and generated $10 million in annual savings. Advantage angle: the winner is not the company with the flashiest model; it is the one that wires prediction into dock doors, route balance, maintenance, exception handling, and customer promises. [Supply Chain Dive, Apr. 1]

- **Robotics adoption beyond mega-shippers.** Supply Chain Dive reported that RaaS/SaaS models and peer proof points are widening warehouse robotics adoption among small and midsized enterprises. A cited MHI/Peerless/Robotics Group study found 48% of surveyed organizations used robots in plants and/or warehouses in 2025, up from 23% three years earlier; more than half of respondents had less than $50 million in annual revenue. Advantage angle: robotics is becoming a variable-cost capacity lever, but only where process fit and throughput economics are real. [Supply Chain Dive, Mar. 2]

- **Tariff refund operations as margin recovery.** CAPE refunds may become a practical advantage for companies with clean entry data and broker discipline. Slow filers leave cash trapped; clean filers can turn legal reversals into working-capital recovery.
