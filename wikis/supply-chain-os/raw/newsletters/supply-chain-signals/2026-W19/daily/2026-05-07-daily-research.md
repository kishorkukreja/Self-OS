# Supply Chain Signals Daily Research — 2026-05-07

## Top Moves Candidates

1. **The cleanest signal today is cost pressure returning through physical constraints, not demand collapse.** S&P Global's April global manufacturing PMI commentary says producer input costs rose at the fastest pace since June 2022 and supplier lead times lengthened the most since August 2022. ISM's April U.S. manufacturing read is consistent with that: PMI held at 52.7, prices paid reached 84.6, and supplier deliveries rose to 60.6. For operators, the issue is margin and lead-time control. Demand can look healthy while the cost model breaks underneath it. [S1][S2][S3]

2. **Services are not immune to the same logistics drag.** ISM Services PMI came in at 53.6 in April, still expanding for a 22nd straight month, but new orders slowed sharply from 60.6 to 53.5, employment stayed in contraction, prices held at 70.7, and supplier deliveries slowed for a 17th consecutive month. Transportation delays, oil-linked inflation, tariffs, and labor constraints showed up in respondent comments. This matters because supply-chain inflation is not confined to factories; it is leaking into wholesale, transportation, construction, health care, food service, and other service networks. [S4]

3. **AI infrastructure has moved from chip shortage story to power-and-equipment bottleneck story.** The IEA reported that data centre electricity demand rose 17% in 2025, while overall global electricity demand grew 3%. It also said capex by five large technology companies exceeded $400 billion in 2025 and is expected to rise another 75% in 2026. The bottleneck list is very physical: gas turbines, transformers, advanced chips, IT components, grid connections, planning approvals, and permits. This strengthens the Wafer to Rack chain for Sunday: the rack is becoming an energy and construction supply chain as much as a semiconductor one. [S5]

4. **Bloomberg's May 6 semiconductor piece gives Wafer to Rack a timely executive hook.** Bloomberg reported that companies and governments are rethinking where chips are made as AI demand pushes semiconductors toward a trillion-dollar industry in 2026. The extract is partly paywalled and Bloomberg AI-generated takeaways are visible, so use it carefully: it is strong for the headline that semiconductor supply chains are being remade, weaker for detailed factual claims unless corroborated elsewhere. [S6]

5. **Tariffs are now a landed-cost operating system, not a one-off surcharge.** FreightWaves reported Maersk's estimate that U.S. importers are paying an effective average tariff rate of about 21% on containerized imports, down from a 54% peak after the April 2 tariff package. Maersk also said visibility worsened and trade barriers increased after April 2. The operator takeaway: sourcing, pricing, routing, and inventory decisions need a live tariff assumption. Static annual cost models will miss the margin risk. [S7]

6. **Packaging is a useful microcosm for why reshoring is harder than the slogan.** Supply Chain Dive reported that packaging supply chains are being hit by tariffs, the Iran war, oil-linked plastics costs, and shipping rates. The article's useful operating point is that switching packaging is slow: brands need material availability, supplier capacity, quality qualification, plant testing, distribution testing, and consumer acceptance. A domestic quote is not the same as a qualified packaging chain. [S8]

7. **Retail tariff mitigation is becoming measurable in gross-margin guidance.** Supply Chain Dive reported that PVH expects to offset 60% of tariff financial impact in 2026, double its 2025 mitigation rate, while assuming $195 million in gross tariff costs and planning around a 15% baseline duty rate. This is a good example for the newsletter's cost-center-to-advantage section: tariff mitigation is moving from procurement firefighting into finance guidance, vendor strategy, sourcing architecture, and margin planning. [S9]

8. **Global trade remains resilient, but the forecast is narrower and more shock-sensitive.** WTO's March 2026 outlook forecast merchandise trade volume growth slowing from 4.6% in 2025 to 1.9% in 2026. It said sustained high oil prices tied to the Middle East conflict could shave 0.5 percentage points from that 2026 merchandise trade forecast, while strength in AI-related goods could add 0.5 percentage points. That is the week's macro tension in one line: AI goods are propping up trade while energy and transport shocks threaten it. [S10]

## Problem Beneath the Headline Candidates

### Problem 1: Inventory buffers are being rebuilt for the wrong kind of volatility

S&P Global says April manufacturing strength was partly boosted by safety-stock building as companies rushed to secure supply before costs rose further. That can protect service in the short term, but it risks creating the familiar hangover: excess inventory in some SKUs, shortages in constrained components, and slower output later when the stock build unwinds.

- **Impact:** working capital tied up in blunt buffers, harder S&OP tradeoffs, uneven service, and delayed recognition of demand weakness.
- **Operator question:** which buffers are tied to named constraints, and which are just anxiety inventory?

### Problem 2: AI demand is turning infrastructure procurement into a race for non-chip capacity

The IEA's constraint list makes the point. The bottleneck is not just GPU supply. It is transformers, gas turbines, grid queues, cooling, construction, permits, and batteries. A cloud buyer can win chip allocation and still lose the deployment schedule on power equipment or interconnection.

- **Impact:** delayed data center commissioning, capex inflation, higher prepayment/reservation needs, more competition with utilities and industrial projects.
- **Operator question:** where is the true critical path: silicon, rack integration, power gear, grid connection, cooling, or site approval?

### Problem 3: Tariff mitigation is creating a second planning layer

PVH's and Maersk's tariff signals show that duty exposure now sits beside demand, capacity, and freight in the planning stack. Teams need to model effective rates, pause deadlines, refund assumptions, supplier country mix, first-sale valuation risk, and customer price pass-through.

- **Impact:** more volatile gross margin, supplier churn, pricing lag, customs/compliance workload, and scenario-planning fatigue.
- **Operator question:** who owns the effective-duty-rate assumption: procurement, finance, trade compliance, or planning?

## From Cost Center to Advantage Candidates

### Treat planning as a constraint-discovery system

BCG's planning report is useful because it cuts against the easy AI story. It argues that AI does not fix weak planning foundations; the advantage comes from how planners apply APS, AI, governance, data, workflows, and decision rights under pressure. Pair that with this week's signals: supplier deliveries slowing, effective tariffs changing, data center power equipment tightening, and packaging substitutions requiring qualification.

Operator takeaway: the planning function should be able to answer three questions quickly: what changed, where is the constraint, and what decision needs to move now? Tools help, but only if decision rights and data are clean enough to act. [S11]

## Notes

Today's strongest thread is **constraint migration**. The constraint is not staying in one place. It moves from tariff rates to routing, from routing to fuel, from fuel to supplier lead times, from chips to transformers, from sourcing alternatives to qualification capacity. Sunday's brief should avoid a generic "volatility is high" argument. The sharper point is that operating advantage now comes from detecting where the active constraint has moved and changing the next decision before the P&L shows the damage.
