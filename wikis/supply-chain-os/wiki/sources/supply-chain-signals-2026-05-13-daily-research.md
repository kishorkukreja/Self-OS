## Summary

The 13 May daily research file distills the day's operator-facing supply-chain narrative. Its lead claim is that freight signals are bifurcating: truckload capacity, rates, and tender rejections show tightening while ocean/import demand remains weak or uncertain after tariff-driven pull-forward. The research then broadens cost pressure beyond oil and freight by using PMI and ISM details to show supplier lead times, input costs, and service-sector delivery delays moving together. The practical implication is that procurement and operations teams should separate scarcity-driven price increases from commodity-driven price increases.

The file also flags two structural themes. First, AI infrastructure is becoming a supply-chain problem, with advanced chips, high-bandwidth memory, packaging, networking, power gear, cooling, and data-center construction forming the critical path. Second, warehouse automation is shifting from bolt-on robotics toward facility design and workflow architecture. For the wiki, this source updates [[concepts/freight-market-disruption.md]], [[concepts/supplier-risk-monitoring.md]], and [[concepts/ai-infrastructure-supply-chain.md]] with a current newsletter synthesis rather than confidential client detail.

## Key contributions
- Frames 2026 freight as a split signal between domestic capacity tightness and fragile ocean/import demand.
- Connects input inflation to supplier lead times and scarcity, not only energy or headline freight rates.
- Elevates AI infrastructure and warehouse automation as supply-chain design constraints.
