## Summary

The 2026-05-06 daily research note frames the week around volatility becoming operating behavior. U.S. manufacturing remains in expansion at a 52.7 PMI, but the internals are uncomfortable: prices paid, supplier deliveries, and customer inventories suggest margin pressure and service risk even without demand collapse. Tariff volatility has moved into SMB decisions, with surveyed firms changing suppliers, sourcing across multiple regions, and raising prices. Retail import forecasts add another complication: tariff pull-forward inventory can make port volumes look weak while fuel, routing, and policy risks keep landed cost unstable.

The note’s strongest strategic thread is “Wafer to Rack.” AI infrastructure demand is not only a chip story; it is reserving memory, wafers, packaging, CPUs/GPUs, cooling, power equipment, data-center logistics, and site capacity. That chain-of-the-week angle connects Reuters memory-shortage reporting, Intel supply constraints, WTO macro trade forecasts, and World Bank commentary on AI-linked goods trade. The final section pairs Gartner’s agentic-AI SCM software forecast with BCG’s warning that AI planning tools only work on top of strong process foundations. The operator takeaway is clear: under continuous volatility, companies need faster live cost assumptions, operationally qualified alternate suppliers, capacity-reservation discipline, and planning decision systems that improve real allocation, expedite, substitution, and sourcing choices.

## Key takeaways

- Volatility is shifting from boardroom scenario planning into day-to-day supplier, price, and inventory actions.
- The Wafer-to-Rack chain highlights how AI demand moves constraints across memory, fabs, packaging, power, cooling, and logistics.
- Agentic AI in planning should be evaluated as a decision-system upgrade, not a shortcut around data and process maturity.
