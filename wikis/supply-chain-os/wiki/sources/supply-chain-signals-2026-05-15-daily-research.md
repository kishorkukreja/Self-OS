## Summary

The May 15 Supply Chain Signals daily research frames current operator risk around synchronized AI infrastructure bottlenecks, uneven food inflation, manufacturing price pressure, and trade-compliance drag. The strongest theme is that wafer-to-rack execution is no longer a narrow semiconductor story. Memory, advanced packaging, wafer starts, data-center equipment, power, cooling, networking, and commissioning are converging into one capacity cycle, creating execution risk for buyers even if market valuations cool.

The research also distinguishes headline cost signals from operational bottlenecks. Food inflation is uneven across oils, proteins, energy-linked inputs, packaging, and freight rather than a single basket. Ocean rates may soften while inland handoffs still create delays. Tariff attention is shifting toward non-tariff measures: documentation, testing, certifications, supplier data, and market-access transparency. For the wiki, this source updates the Supply Chain Signals corpus with an operator-first map: ask which constraint is binding, which handoff is failing, and which decision actually gets faster when vendors claim AI-enabled execution.

## Key contributions
- AI infrastructure demand is a synchronized supply-chain bottleneck across chips, power, cooling, and construction.
- Food and manufacturing cost pressure should be read at the SKU/input and supplier-delivery level, not only the headline index.
- Compliance work and inland execution can dominate cost and service even when tariff or ocean-rate headlines improve.
