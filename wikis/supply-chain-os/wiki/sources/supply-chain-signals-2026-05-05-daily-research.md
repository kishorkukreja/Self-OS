## Summary

Tariff risk is back in the operating plan, not just the legal file. Reuters reported that German automaker shares fell after President Trump said U.S. tariffs on imported European cars would rise to 25%, up from a previously agreed 15% levy. Bernstein estimated the extra 10 percentage points could cost German automakers about €2.6 billion in operating profit this year. Operator angle: tariff volatility is now a product line profitability problem, a sourcing problem, and a footprint problem at the same time. Watch whether exposed European premium brands accelerate U.S. localization or push more price increases into a softer demand environment. manufacturing is expanding, but the supply side is getting tight again. Reuters and ISM reported April Manufacturing PMI at 52.7, unchanged from March and the fourth consecutive month of expansion. Under the surface, supplier deliveries slowed faster, prices paid jumped to 84.6, and customers' inventories stayed too low at 39.1. This is the kind of PMI mix operators hate: demand has not collapsed, but inputs are more expensive, deliveries are slower, and customer buffers are thin. Fuel has become the hidden tax on container freight. West Coast rates rising even during a normally soft season. One Freightos Baltic Index snapshot had West Coast rates up 7% to $2,653 per FEU and East Coast rates up 4% to $3,810 per FEU; a later FreightWaves item put West Coast rates at $2,675 per FEU and East Coast at $3,939 per FEU. Demand is not strong enough to explain the move. Fuel and war related surcharges are doing much of the work. AI infrastructure is running into industrial supply chains. Manufacturing Dive, citing Omdia, framed the 2026 semiconductor bottleneck as a shortage of physical inputs rather than chip design: power, copper, helium, bromine, HBM, grid connections, transformers, and data center cooling. The article reported that hyperscalers have committed more than $660 billion in 2026 capex, while AI data centers can require 100 500 MW per facility and grid connections can take three to seven years. Food costs are reacting to the same energy shock as freight. FAO's March Food Price Index rose 2.4% month on month to 128.5, the second consecutive increase. FAO tied the move mostly to energy related pressure on vegetable oil and sugar prices, with all five commodity groups up. The food chain is another reminder that energy shocks travel through packaging, transport, fertilizer, biofuels, and consumer price sensitivity. Global trade is slower, but AI goods are carrying more of the load. WTO's March 2026 outlook forecast merchandise trade volume growth slowing from 4.6% in 2025 to 1.

## Key takeaways

- Impact: margin leakage, late repricing, expediting, higher working capital, weaker service levels.
- Operator question: which assumptions in the operating plan are now too slow: duty rates, bunker fuel, lead times, supplier allocations, or customer demand?
- Impact: capex delay, allocation fights, longer qualification cycles, and higher costs for non AI industrial buyers.
- Operator question: which second order inputs would stop your growth plan even if tier one suppliers say they can ship?
