## Summary

Trump hikes EU auto tariffs to 25%, escalating transatlantic trade friction What happened: On May 1, President Trump announced he would raise tariffs on cars and trucks from the European Union to 25% next week, up from the previously agreed 15% under the August 2025 trade deal. Trump cited EU non compliance with the deal; the European Commission rejected the claim and said it would keep options open to protect EU interests. The announcement hit on May Day, as the EU Mercosur trade deal launched. Why it matters: Auto tariffs are a precision strike on Germany's export engine and a test of whether the EU will retaliate or accelerate alternative trade pacts. Shares of Ford, Stellantis, and GM fell on the news. For supply chain operators, this is another signal that tariff rates are becoming a moving target tied to political moments rather than negotiated schedules. What to watch: EU retaliation threats, automaker production shift announcements, and whether the 25% rate sticks or becomes a negotiation lever. Sources: Reuters, May 1, 2026; AP News, May 1, 2026. ISM Manufacturing PMI holds at 52.7% but prices surge to 84.6% What happened: The April 2026 ISM Manufacturing PMI registered 52.7%, unchanged from March and marking four consecutive months of expansion. But the Prices Index jumped 6.3 points to 84.6% — its highest since April 2022 — and Supplier Deliveries slowed further to 60.6%. Employment remained in contraction at 46.4%, with 60% of panelists managing headcounts rather than hiring. Why it matters: Factories are growing output while facing the worst input cost inflation in four years and refusing to add labor. That is a margin squeeze in real time. The Iran war and tariffs were mentioned in 65% of panel comments, with the war cited in 47% and tariffs in 18%. What to watch: May PMI for whether prices keep climbing, and whether employment contraction deepens into a broader industrial slowdown. Source: ISM Manufacturing PMI Report, April 2026. McKinsey: AI trade drove one third of global trade growth in 2025; US China flows dropped ~30% What happened: McKinsey Global Institute's March 2026 update on global trade geometry finds that semiconductors and data center equipment accounted for roughly one third of global trade growth in 2025. Despite tariff fears, US imports and Chinese exports both reached new highs. However, direct US China bilateral trade fell by around 30%, with the US replacing about two thirds of the gap from other sources and Chinese exporters cutting prices by an average of 8% to find new markets.
