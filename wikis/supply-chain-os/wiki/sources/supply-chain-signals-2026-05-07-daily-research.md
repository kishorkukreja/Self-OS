## Summary

The 2026-05-07 daily research note develops a clear editorial thesis: the active constraint is migrating across the supply chain rather than staying in one obvious bottleneck. PMI data shows producer input costs and supplier lead times worsening while manufacturing still expands. Services show similar friction through transportation delays, energy-linked inflation, tariffs, and labor constraints. AI infrastructure shifts from a semiconductor shortage story to a power-equipment and permitting story, with IEA data on data-center electricity growth and bottlenecks in turbines, transformers, grid connections, and approvals.

The note's operating value is its translation of news into planning questions. Inventory buffers may be rebuilt as anxiety stock rather than named-constraint protection. Tariffs become a second planning layer that affects effective duty rates, supplier country mix, pricing, compliance, and finance guidance. Packaging becomes a practical example of why reshoring and supplier switching require qualification capacity, not just alternate quotes. The preferred “Wafer to Rack” chain of the week remains useful because it frames AI infrastructure as a full physical supply chain ending only when a rack is installed, powered, cooled, connected, and serviceable. This source should feed [[concepts/constraint-migration]], [[concepts/tariff-planning-layer]], and [[concepts/supply-chain-planning-discipline]].

## Key takeaways

- Cost pressure, lead-time risk, tariffs, energy capacity, and supplier qualification need to be modeled together rather than as separate news items.
- Primary metrics should be traced back to ISM, S&P Global, IEA, WTO, carrier, or company disclosures before charting.
- The strongest planning theme is constraint discovery: identify where the current bottleneck has moved before the P&L impact is visible.
