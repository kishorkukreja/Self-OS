## Summary

The 2026-05-08 daily research note sharpens the week’s editorial thesis: the supply-chain constraint keeps migrating, and operators need a live map of the active bottleneck. Food inflation, energy costs, fertilizer affordability, manufacturing prices, services input costs, and trade forecasts are treated as connected signals rather than separate macro headlines. The note highlights FAO food price movement, Fed comments on supply-chain inflation, ISM manufacturing and services pressure, WTO trade forecasts, and World Bank commodity forecasts to show a synchronized cost shock across energy, food, manufacturing, and services.

The note also adds two strategic platform stories. Amazon Supply Chain Services is framed as a cost-center-to-advantage move: Amazon is opening freight, fulfillment, warehousing, forecasting, and delivery capacity to outside businesses. AMD's AI infrastructure demand update reinforces the [[concepts/wafer-to-rack-supply-chain]] theme by expanding the bill of materials from GPUs into CPUs, memory, networking, power, cooling, construction, and service logistics.

The operational contribution is the call for a [[concepts/live-constraint-map]]. Static risk registers are too slow when constraints move from tariffs to fuel, supplier deliveries, food inputs, AI server CPUs, power infrastructure, or logistics capacity inside the same week. The useful management question is who owns the current constraint and what buying, buffering, routing, pricing, or supplier decision changes because of it.

## Key takeaways

- Energy, fertilizer, food, manufacturing input costs, and services delivery pressure should be modeled together.
- Amazon's logistics platform move turns network density and forecasting into a strategic product.
- AI infrastructure demand keeps broadening the physical supply chain from chips to racks, power, cooling, and site execution.
