## Summary

Supply Chain Signals — 2026-W18 The big signal This week’s supply-chain story is cash, not chaos. The familiar disruption headlines are all here: Hormuz, tariffs, port dwell, air rates, slower supplier deliveries, AI-chip constraints. But the pattern underneath is sharper. Supply-chain decisions are turning into capital-allocation decisions faster than most operating rhythms can handle. A customs error now delays tariff refunds. A wrong inventory buffer ties up cash while the constrained SKU still misses service. A reroute changes not only lead time but landed cost, customer priority, and margin. A data-center build no longer depends only on chips; it depends on power equipment, grid access, cooling, optical modules, permits, and logistics landing in the same window. The companies that do well in this environment will not be the ones with the prettiest visibility dashboards. They will be the ones that can make specific decisions quickly: which orders to protect, which lanes to abandon, which entries to fix, which suppliers deserve scarce capacity, and which customers need a hard conversation before the miss happens. Resilience is too vague a word for this. The useful capability is decision quality under constraint. This week gave operators plenty of constraints. Top moves this week 1. Manufacturing expanded, but prices and delivery times sent the warning U.S. manufacturing stayed in expansion in April. Reuters and ISM both reported the Manufacturing PMI at 52.7%, unchanged from March and above 50 for the fourth straight month. New Orders improved to 54.1%. That is the good news. The bad news is the cost side. ISM’s Prices Index hit 84.6%, the highest since April 2022. Supplier Deliveries rose to 60.6%, which means deliveries slowed further. Employment stayed in contraction at 46.4%. For operators, this looks less like a clean recovery and more like a margin squeeze with demand still alive. Procurement teams face input inflation. Planners face longer lead times. Plant leaders are not adding labor. Sales teams may still see orders, but the cost-to-serve math is moving underneath them. What to watch: May PMI, especially prices, supplier deliveries, customer inventories, and export orders. If prices stay elevated while employment contracts, the industrial recovery gets harder to monetize. 2.
