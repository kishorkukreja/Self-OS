## Summary

Supply Chain Signals Daily Research — 2026-05-03 Top Moves Candidates 1. U.S. manufacturing grows, but the cost signal got ugly - What happened: Reuters and ISM report that the April Manufacturing PMI held at 52.7%, its fourth straight month above 50. New Orders improved to 54.1%, but the Prices Index climbed to 84.6%, its highest level since April 2022. Supplier Deliveries rose to 60.6, meaning deliveries slowed further, while Employment stayed in contraction at 46.4. - Why it matters: This is not a clean industrial recovery. Factories are still expanding while input costs, logistics delays, tariffs, and energy shock move against them. Operators should expect margin pressure to show up before volume weakness. - What to watch: May supplier deliveries, customer inventories, export orders, and whether price pressure spills into finished goods faster than procurement teams can re-source or re-price. - Sources: Reuters, May 1, 2026; ISM, May 2026 roundup. 2. Canadian factories are stockpiling because they are worried, not because demand is suddenly strong - What happened: Reuters reports that Canada's S&P Global Manufacturing PMI rose to 53.3 in April, its highest level since June 2022. S&P Global cautioned that the lift appears tied to war-related stock building. Vendor delivery times deteriorated, and input prices reached their highest since August 2022. - Why it matters: Inventory growth caused by fear is a different animal from inventory growth caused by demand. It can protect service for a few weeks and then leave the wrong material in the wrong node. - What to watch: Whether Canadian export orders hold up once urgent stock builds clear, and whether firms separate strategic buffers from nervous buying. - Source: Reuters, May 1, 2026. 3. Hormuz has moved from disruption event to network redesign - What happened: Reuters says the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz affects a shipping lane that handles roughly one-fifth of global daily oil and LNG supply. project44's analysis of the first month after the disruption counted 34,294 route diversions, with Navi Mumbai dwell rising to 23.47 days and transshipment volumes up more than 700% versus February daily averages. - Why it matters: This is the week’s clearest operator signal.
