## Book positioning

**AI Engineering** focuses on the end-to-end process of adapting **foundation models** to solve real-world problems. Foundation models include:

- Large language models (**LLMs**)
- Large multimodal models (**LMMs**)

The book combines:

- established engineering practices,
- emerging techniques specific to foundation models,
- practical production lessons,
- case studies and real-world examples,
- evaluation, deployment, optimization, and feedback-loop concerns.

Chip explicitly positions the book around fundamentals:

> Tools become outdated quickly, but fundamentals should last longer.

It is not primarily a tutorial/code-snippet book. It is a technical framework for evaluating and adapting foundation models for real applications.

## Chapter summary highlights

### Chapter 1 — Introduction to Building AI Applications with Foundation Models

Introduces **AI engineering** as a discipline made possible by foundation models. It covers the transition from language models to LLMs, then to multimodal foundation models and AI engineering. It emphasizes deciding **whether an AI application should be built** before building it, and compares AI engineering with ML engineering and full-stack engineering.

Common generative AI use cases include coding, image/video production, writing, education, conversational bots, information aggregation, data organization, and workflow automation.

### Chapter 2 — Understanding Foundation Models

Explains training data, multilingual/domain-specific models, model architecture, model size, post-training, supervised finetuning, preference finetuning, sampling, test-time compute, structured outputs, and the probabilistic nature of AI systems.

### Chapter 3 — Evaluation Methodology

Covers how to evaluate foundation models and systems, including entropy, cross-entropy, bits-per-character/byte, perplexity, exact evaluation, functional correctness, similarity measures, embeddings, AI-as-judge, limitations of judge models, and comparative evaluation.

### Chapter 4 — Evaluate AI Systems

Focuses on evaluating complete AI applications rather than isolated models. This matters for Self-OS because agent and RAG systems often fail at the system level even when individual model outputs look plausible.

### Chapter 5 — Prompt Engineering

Covers prompt engineering practices, prompt examples, and defensive prompting. The repo’s `prompt-examples.md` includes real prompts from production-like settings such as Brex, Cursor, Grab, Pinterest, Thoughtworks, and Whatnot.

### Chapter 6 — RAG and Agents

Covers retrieval-augmented generation and agent systems. This is one of the most relevant sections for Self-OS, because the project is heavily concerned with agents, context construction, knowledge retrieval, and evaluation.

### Chapter 7 — Finetuning

Covers when and how to adapt models through finetuning, including cases where finetuning is not the right answer.

### Chapter 8 — Dataset Engineering

Covers data quality, dataset construction, and public datasets. This is relevant for evaluation sets, fine-tuning datasets, and agent memory/knowledge-base quality.

### Chapter 9 — Inference Optimization

Covers making AI systems faster, cheaper, and more efficient. This aligns with cost-aware agent operation, local/open model decisions, context efficiency, and production latency tradeoffs.

### Chapter 10 — AI Engineering Architecture and User Feedback

Covers production architecture and feedback loops. This is relevant to Self-OS as an operating layer: building systems that learn from interactions, collect feedback, and improve continuously.

## Prompt examples

`prompt-examples.md` collects prompt examples from actual use cases and hypothetical examples demonstrating prompt engineering techniques.

Real-world examples include:

- **Brex:** financial assistant prompt with user/location/time/inbox context, concise tone, and style constraints.
- **Cursor:** task-or-not classifier that outputs exactly `EDITOR TASK` or `NOT EDITOR TASK`, with explicit edge-case handling.
- **Grab:** database column/entity classifier with tag definitions and negative examples.
- **Pinterest:** text-to-SQL prompt examples.
- **Thoughtworks:** copilot for product ideation.
- **Whatnot:** content moderation.

Actionable patterns:

- Provide environment/user context when relevant.
- Define role and task boundaries explicitly.
- For classifiers, use a tight label space.
- Clarify edge cases and non-goals.
- Include examples and negative examples for precision.
- Constrain output format when the downstream system needs machine readability.
